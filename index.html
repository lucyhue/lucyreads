The Thursday Murder Club

In a peaceful retirement village, four unlikely friends meet weekly in the Jigsaw Room to discuss unsolved crimes; together they call themselves The Thursday Murder Club. Elizabeth, Joyce, Ibrahim and Ron might be pushing eighty but they still have a few tricks up their sleeves.

When a local developer is found dead with a mysterious photograph left next to the body, the Thursday Murder Club suddenly find themselves in the middle of their first live case. As the bodies begin to pile up, can our unorthodox but brilliant gang catch the killer, before it’s too late?

MAY 2021 REVIEW

Hamnet by Maggie O'Farrell

I loved this book! From the very first chapter, I just felt like I was there in Stratford watching the characters’ lives. I found the descriptions so detailed (but not in a long, dragging way) that I was able to picture the whole thing, which I loved! What a wonderful read.

Hamnet follows the lives of William Shakespeare’s family. Shakespeare is never named in the book, only being referred to as “the father”, “the husband” and sometimes, “the Latin teacher”. Much of the book focuses on Agnes, his wife, a woman who is unconventional for the period, has an extraordinary relationship with nature and a somewhat mystical sixth sense. I found this in particularly a fascinating part of her character and enjoyed how Maggie O’Farrell used it in the text to introduce and explain various events. It is her sixth sense that alerts Agnes to Shakespeare’s state of being as he struggles to settle to work as a glover in Stratford. She describes his depression as a dreadful smell coming off him, which I found to be a very clever way to explain it and how it impacted their lives.

APRIL 2021 - LUCY'S REVIEW

Red at the Bone by Jacqueline Woodson

The book is told from the perspectives of all Melody’s family members. I wasn’t expecting Aubrey, Melody’s father, to be a presence in the book as he isn’t mentioned on the blurb and my expectation (rightly or wrongly) was that Iris would be a single parent. I found Aubrey a beautiful character and loved his bond with his daughter, as he protected her and worked hard for her, even within a family that wasn’t totally his own. Melody’s grandparents, Sabe and Po’Boy were wise, protective and, at times, comical. Their acceptance of Iris’ pregnancy, their willingness to uproot their whole lives to protect the family from the cruel words of others and their total love of Melody were endearing. I just wanted to hear more stories about their experiences and time with their family.

Overall, an interesting and speedy read, but one that’s left me wanting more.
