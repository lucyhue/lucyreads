JULY 2021 REVEALED

The Foundling by Stacey Halls

Two women, bound by a child, and a secret that will change everything...

London, 1754. Six years after leaving her illegitimate daughter Clara at London's Foundling Hospital, Bess Bright returns to reclaim the child she has never known. Dreading the worst, that Clara has died in care, Bess is astonished to be told she has already claimed her. Her life is turned upside down as she tries to find out who has taken her little girl - and why.

Less than a mile from Bess's lodgings in the city, in a quiet, gloomy townhouse on the edge of London, a young widow has not left the house in a decade. When her close friend - an ambitious young doctor at the Foundling Hospital - persuades her to hire a nursemaid for her daughter, she is hesitant to welcome someone new into her home and her life. But her past is threatening to catch up with her and tear her carefully constructed world apart. From the bestselling author of The Familiars comes this captivating story of mothers and daughters, class and power, and love against the greatest of odds ...

JUNE 2021 REVIEW

The Thursday Murder Club by Richard Osman

I’m sure I’m not the only one who thinks that living in Coopers Chase sounds like a lot of fun! Richard Osman creates some wonderful characters in his debut novel. Joyce, Elizabeth, Ibrahim and Ron are members of the Thursday Murder Club and enjoy puzzling out unsolved murders, when they find one within their community. They may seem like an unlikely group of detectives, but the characters prove that you’re never too old to solve a crime!

MAY 2021 REVIEW

Hamnet by Maggie O'Farrell

I loved this book! From the very first chapter, I just felt like I was there in Stratford watching the characters’ lives. I found the descriptions so detailed (but not in a long, dragging way) that I was able to picture the whole thing, which I loved! What a wonderful read.

Hamnet follows the lives of William Shakespeare’s family. Shakespeare is never named in the book, only being referred to as “the father”, “the husband” and sometimes, “the Latin teacher”. Much of the book focuses on Agnes, his wife, a woman who is unconventional for the period, has an extraordinary relationship with nature and a somewhat mystical sixth sense. I found this in particularly a fascinating part of her character and enjoyed how Maggie O’Farrell used it in the text to introduce and explain various events. It is her sixth sense that alerts Agnes to Shakespeare’s state of being as he struggles to settle to work as a glover in Stratford. She describes his depression as a dreadful smell coming off him, which I found to be a very clever way to explain it and how it impacted their lives.
