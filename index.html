AUGUST 2021 REVEALED

The Beekeeper of Aleppo by Christy Lefteri

The unforgettable love story of a mother blinded by loss and her husband who insists on their survival as they undertake the Syrian refugee trail to Europe.

Nuri is a beekeeper; his wife, Afra, an artist. They live a simple life, rich in family and friends, in the beautiful Syrian city of Aleppo--until the unthinkable happens. When all they care for is destroyed by war, they are forced to escape. But what Afra has seen is so terrible she has gone blind, and so they must embark on a perilous journey through Turkey and Greece towards an uncertain future in Britain. On the way, Nuri is sustained by the knowledge that waiting for them is Mustafa, his cousin and business partner, who has started an apiary and is teaching fellow refugees in Yorkshire to keep bees.

As Nuri and Afra travel through a broken world, they must confront not only the pain of their own unspeakable loss, but dangers that would overwhelm the bravest of souls. Above all, they must journey to find each other again.

Moving, powerful, compassionate, and beautifully written, The Beekeeper of Aleppo is a testament to the triumph of the human spirit. It is the kind of book that reminds us of the power of storytelling.

JULY 2021 REVIEW

The Foundling by Stacey Halls

I really enjoyed The Foundling!

This book is all about motherhood and the different ways one can be a mother. You may think that Bess giving up her child to the Foundling Hospital on the day she was born makes her a poor mother, but she fights for over six years to get her back. I found it incredibly sad to think that she and her family were living in such poverty despite working six days a week that they could not afford to keep the baby. On the other hand, Alexandra can give her everything; she has a warm bed, good food and lots of toys. Nevertheless, she lacks the warmth of a mother’s love and cannot give Charlotte the experiences she craves. I feel that Stacey Halls guides the reader to feel that what Bess can give is superior, despite her lack of material goods, but we are never led to hate Alexandra. I felt that the ending to the book, although rather predictable and a bit cheesy, allowed both women to be the best mothers they could be, and I felt that Charlotte was a very lucky girl, to have the best from both.

LUCY'S 2021 FAVE SO FAR

Hamnet by Maggie O'Farrell

I loved this book! From the very first chapter, I just felt like I was there in Stratford watching the characters’ lives. I found the descriptions so detailed (but not in a long, dragging way) that I was able to picture the whole thing, which I loved! What a wonderful read.

Hamnet follows the lives of William Shakespeare’s family. Shakespeare is never named in the book, only being referred to as “the father”, “the husband” and sometimes, “the Latin teacher”. Much of the book focuses on Agnes, his wife, a woman who is unconventional for the period, has an extraordinary relationship with nature and a somewhat mystical sixth sense. I found this in particularly a fascinating part of her character and enjoyed how Maggie O’Farrell used it in the text to introduce and explain various events. It is her sixth sense that alerts Agnes to Shakespeare’s state of being as he struggles to settle to work as a glover in Stratford. She describes his depression as a dreadful smell coming off him, which I found to be a very clever way to explain it and how it impacted their lives.
