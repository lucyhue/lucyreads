JULY 2020

A month dedicated to black authors and the BLM movement

Books give us the opportunity to step into someone else’s shoes and see the world from their angle. With increased empathy comes an increased responsibility to ensure we are acting fairly and justly, and to ensure that race has no bearing on how we treat people.

I know that in order to become race blind, we need to be race aware, which is why in July, Lucy Reads will host a free-choice month dedicated to black authors and the BLM movement with eight incredible titles to choose from. No matter which one/ones you pick, we'd love to read your review.

Americanah by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

As teenagers in Lagos, Ifemelu and Obinze fall in love. Their Nigeria is under military dictatorship, and people are fleeing the country if they can. The self-assured Ifemelu departs for America. There she suffers defeats and triumphs, finds and loses relationships, all the while feeling the weight of something she never thought of back home: race. Obinze had hoped to join her, but post-9/11 America will not let him in, and he plunges into a dangerous, undocumented life in London.

Thirteen years later, Obinze is a wealthy man in a newly democratic Nigeria, while Ifemelu has achieved success as a blogger. But after so long apart and so many changes, will they find the courage to meet again, face to face?

On Beauty by Zadie Smith

Set in New England mainly and London partly, On Beauty concerns a pair of feuding families - the Belseys and the Kipps - and a clutch of doomed affairs. It puts low morals among high ideals and asks some searching questions about what life does to love.

For the Belseys and the Kipps, the confusions - both personal and political - of our uncertain age are about to be brought close to home: right to the heart of family.

Queenie by Candice Carty-Williams

Caught between the Jamaican British family who don’t seem to understand her, a job that’s not all it promised and a man she just can’t get over, Queenie’s life seems to be steadily spiralling out of control. Desperately trying to navigate her way through a hot mess of shifting cultures and toxic relationships and emerge with a shred of dignity, her missteps and misadventures will provoke howls of laughter and tears of pity – frequently on the same page.

Between the World and Me by Ta-Nehisi Coates

An attempt to answer questions about race in America, in the form of a letter to the author's 14-year-old son.

Coates takes readers along on his journey through America's history of race and its contemporary resonances through a series of awakenings - moments when he discovered some new truth about our long, tangled history of race, whether through his myth-busting professors at Howard University, a trip to a Civil War battlefield, a journey to Chicago's South Side to visit aging survivors of 20th century America's "long war on black people," or a visit with the mother of a beloved friend who was shot down by the police.

Girl, Woman, Other by Bernadine Evaristo

Girl, Woman, Other follows the lives and struggles of twelve very different characters. Mostly women, black and British, they tell the stories of their families, friends and lovers, across the country and through the years.

Joyfully polyphonic and vibrantly contemporary, this is a gloriously new kind of history, a novel of our times: celebratory, ever-dynamic and utterly irresistible.

Stay with Me by Ayobami Adebayo

Yejide is hoping for a miracle, for a child.

It is all her husband wants, all her mother-in-law wants, and she has tried everything - arduous pilgrimages, medical consultations, dances with prophets, appeals to God. But when her in-laws insist upon a new wife, it is too much for Yejide to bear. It will lead to jealousy, betrayal and despair.

The Underground Railroad by Colson Whitehead

Cora is a slave on a cotton plantation in Georgia. All the slaves lead a hellish existence, but Cora has it worse than most; she is an outcast even among her fellow Africans and she is approaching womanhood, where it is clear even greater pain awaits. When Caesar, a slave recently arrived from Virginia, tells her about the Underground Railroad, they take the perilous decision to escape to the North.

Homegoing by Yaa Gyasi

Effia and Esi: two sisters with two very different destinies. One sold into slavery; one a slave trader's wife.

The consequences of their fate reverberate through the generations that follow. As each chapter offers up a new descendant, alternating between Effia’s and Esi’s bloodline right up to the present day, a chasm of experience and the differing legacies of chance are brought starkly to light.

JUNE 2020

A Woman is No Man by Etaf Rum

Three generations of Palestinian-American women living in Brooklyn are torn between individual desire and the strict mores of Arab culture in this heart-wrenching story of love, intrigue and courage.

Palestine, 1990. Seventeen-year-old Isra prefers reading books to entertaining the suitors her father has chosen for her. Over the course of a week, the naive and dreamy girl finds herself quickly betrothed and married, and is soon living in Brooklyn. There Isra struggles to adapt to the expectations of her oppressive mother-in-law Fareeda and strange new husband Adam, a pressure that intensifies as she begins to have children - four daughters instead of the sons Fareeda tells Isra she must bear.

Brooklyn, 2008. Eighteen-year-old Deya, Isra's oldest daughter, must meet with potential husbands at her grandmother Fareeda's insistence, though her only desire is to go to college. But her grandmother is firm on the matter: the only way to secure a worthy future for Deya is through marriage to the right man.

But fate has a will of its own, and soon Deya will find herself on an unexpected path that leads her to shocking truths about her family...


WHY PICK THIS BOOK?

When I read the blurb of this book, I was immediately reminded of one of my favourite books from my schooldays, A Thousand Splendid Suns by Khaled Hosseini. I then saw a couple of reviews, which made the same link, so I am hopeful that this is going to be a similarly powerful book.

Dealing with three generations of women and the family and cultural pressures they face, Etaf Rum gives a voice to the previously unheard.


LUCY'S REVIEW

My initial response to the subject matter of this book is one of disbelief; I find it hard to imagine that women are suffering like Isra around the world in the ways described in this book, and yet that is why Etaf Rum has written it. The shockingly recent dates, with much of the book set in the 1990s and 2000s, confirm that Rum isn’t talking about how things used to be for women in many cultures, but how they still are. A Woman is No Man gives a strong insight into the lives of three generations of women, through the characters of Fareeda, Isra and Deya, and the expectations placed upon them and, very importantly, gives them a voice. Through tackling difficult matters such as arranged marriages, domestic abuse and self-acceptance, this book will hopefully increase reader awareness and give some a mirror through which they may be able to question their own experiences or those around them.

I was aware while reading the book that my own views on arranged marriages were bound to cloud my overall judgement and I knew I would find myself siding with the dispossessed women against their patriarchal society. The book had a strong and single-minded agenda, and I am aware of its portrayal of Arab culture and particularly Arab males in a very negative light. As well as giving some women hope, I do wonder whether there is a risk that many readers will have a renewed negative view of the entire culture, and worry that this may contribute to future xenophobia. Certainly, I cannot see any positives from the portrayal of Arab culture in this book, and the conclusion seems to be very much in favour of Americanism, which feels one sided.

The last chapter was a little unexpected and provided one of the few more nuanced parts of the book. We are never told whether the scene of Isra and her daughters in the train station actually happened and was the final straw that led to Adam acting as he did, or whether this was a fictional new chapter written by Deya for her mother, in which they escaped and moved towards the “happily ever after” ending that Isra had dreamed of. The empowering of Deya not only enabled her to pursue her dreams, by going to college and writing her story, but could therefore have effectively freed Isra, through realising the strength in her character and giving her a new ending. Although Rum doesn’t give us any idea which is the case, this helps add another dimension to the empowerment of women, indicating that it is not too late for older generations to break free, and that the younger generation will help them do it. Perhaps this mirrors Rum’s intention for her book and the impact it will have on all generations of readers. It has certainly left me thinking!


READERS' REVIEWS

Ultimately, it is a harrowing depiction of the mental and physical abuse these women suffer as they obey the religious, cultural and traditional demands of the dominant males in the family. The stigma of good reputation and family honour must be maintained at all times. It was very much that ' a women's place is in her home'.

MAY 2020

Daisy Jones & The Six by Taylor Jenkins Reid

Everyone knows Daisy Jones and The Six.

Their albums were on every turntable, they sold out arenas from coast to coast, their sound defined an era. And then, on 12 July 1979, they split.

Nobody ever knew why. Until now.

The only thing that's certain is that from the moment Daisy Jones walked, barefoot, on to the stage at the Whisky, the band were irrevocably changed...

This is the story of their incredible rise and fall. The ambition, the desire, the heartbreak, and the music.


WHY PICK THIS BOOK?

I've picked this book up a few times, attracted by the cover and the fact that it's about music! Initially, I was unsure of the interview-style, but I read the first few pages and was quickly hooked. This book is a complete contrast from April's and looks to be great fun!


LUCY'S REVIEW

I don’t think I’ve ever read a book quite like this before. At first, I was a little put-off by the interview style, but I actually think this is one of the greatest features of the book. The book has a gossip-mag vibe, where you feel like you are being let into secrets from the band’s shady history and want to keep reading more to see what they all have to say about each dramatic episode. The interviews also allow you to fully get to know each of the individual characters, through hearing their take on a situation, and I quickly felt that I knew who they all were and what they brought to the band. I particularly enjoyed the tension, confusion and, at some points, humour created when two people remembered something differently - when Billy says Daisy was wearing a white t-shirt and then Karen reports that it was a yellow crop top made me smile as their different versions of the same memory matched the personalities of each of the characters.

The fact that it is so hard to tell whether this is actually a true story or fictional (and I did google it to check!) must be one of the greatest achievements of Daisy Jones & The Six. Jenkins Reid has created a wholly believable world of rock ‘n’ roll, with true-to-life characters and a credible rise to fame. However, I do think you have to read this book in as few sittings as possible to get fully into the world she creates and remember who everyone is!

I know that many people have read this book and been drawn into the rockstar lifestyle portrayed, wishing they were one of The Six! I have to say that it didn’t have that effect on me, but I was taken with the individual characters and I enjoyed following them during their rise to fame. I always prefer a plot-driven novel rather than a character-analysis, but nevertheless, I did enjoy Daisy Jones & The Six and am glad I went out of my comfort zone to read it!

Where the Crawdads Sing by Delia Owens

"For years, rumors of the "Marsh Girl" have haunted Barkley Cove, a quiet town on the North Carolina coast. So in late 1969, when handsome Chase Andrews is found dead, the locals immediately suspect Kya Clark, the so-called Marsh Girl. But Kya is not what they say.

Sensitive and intelligent, she has survived for years alone in the marsh that she calls home, finding friends in the gulls and lessons in the sand. Then the time comes when she yearns to be touched and loved. When two young men from town become intrigued by her wild beauty, Kya opens herself to a new life - until the unthinkable happens."

Where the Crawdads Sing is at once an exquisite ode to the natural world, a heartbreaking coming-of-age story, and a surprising tale of possible murder. Owens reminds us that we are forever shaped by the children we once were, and that we are all subject to the beautiful and violent secrets that nature keeps.


WHY PICK THIS BOOK?

This is a book I spotted in the bookshop and was intrigued, partly because I hadn't a clue what crawdads were and partly because I love a mystery to solve! It looks like we are all going to be spending more time indoors in the coming month, so what better time to read a challenging book like Where the Crawdads Sing?


LUCY'S REVIEW

I devoured this book, which is always a sign that it’s a good one!

Kya is a young girl, abandoned by her family, who takes solace in the marsh that is her home. It is easy to understand why she so quickly latches on to anyone who expresses an interest in her, despite their intentions, in a desperate hope for love and acceptance. And it is even easier to understand why, having been left behind so many times, she develops a self-reliance coupled with a lack of trust for others, that will both look after her and keep her from developing meaningful relationships.

I was saddened but maybe not surprised at how most of the white people in the book respond to Kya. The town people reject her for being different, warn their children not to go near her, make up stories about her and blame her for Chase’s death, an easy scapegoat, fuelled by their existing prejudices. This pack mentality, where it is easiest to pick on the “other”, the outsider, is something Owens makes us disapprove of in the book, only to make us question this in the final pages. By the end, I felt that, despite rooting for her the whole way through the book, I had also underestimated her and maybe was as guilty as the other people in the book of not expecting her capable of much.

I’m not going to lie - I had to look up Crawdads on Google - but I now feel that I have a proper appreciation of the beauty of the marsh and a renewed outlook on the natural world. The descriptions are beautiful and so intwined into the narrative that they feel an integral part of the story and not the backdrop it is set against. Some books enable you to create a vision in your head, and this is definitely one of those.

Owens cleverly writes the book in two time frames; the “present day” of the murder investigation, and the childhood of Kya. As we hear more of Kya’s childhood, the two come together without you even noticing. I felt engaged by both periods, and when they aligned, the impetus to turn the page and find out what was going to happen was strong. I loved his book and would recommend it to anyone.

I enjoyed this book and would recommend to anyone looking for something a bit different: this is a book that cannot be easily categorised into a particular genre.

Delia Owens paints an atmospheric picture of the marsh and the world Kya inhabits is realised with vivid detail. MARCH 2020

An American Marriage by Tayari Jones

"Newlyweds Celestial and Roy are the embodiment of the American Dream. He is a young executive, and she is an artist on the brink of an exciting career. Until one day they are ripped apart by circumstances neither could have imagined. Roy is arrested and sentenced to twelve years for a crime Celestial knows he didn't commit.

Devastated and unmoored, Celestial finds herself struggling to hold on to the love that has been her centre, taking comfort in Andre, their closest friend. When Roy's conviction is suddenly overturned, he returns home ready to resume their life together."

A masterpiece of storytelling, An American Marriage offers a profoundly insightful look into the hearts and minds of three unforgettable characters who are at once bound together and separated by forces beyond their control.


WHY PICK THIS BOOK?

When I read what this book is about, I was immediately intrigued by Celestial and Roy's story and wanted to know how it turned out. Emotions are already flying high and I've only got as far as the blurb - this is set to be a moving read.


LUCY'S REVIEW

The blurb of this book tells you that a young couple will be separated early in their marriage, as the husband is put in prison for a crime he didn’t commit. Therefore, there were few surprises, as the story plays out as advertised. How the events affects the couple, and those nearest to them is what makes this book so gripping. The pain they, and all those nearest to them, go through in the book makes the reader reevaluate their priorities and consider the fragility of our lives. At a time when covid-19 is spreading globally, the ease with which your everyday existence can be turned on its head seems entirely relatable, and I felt for all the characters involved as their lives were changed forever.

Hearing the story from three different viewpoints enables the reader to understand how each character has come to their present state and the decisions they have made to get there. This should mean that we feel torn, as we understand each character’s motivations and perspectives. However, although the idea was promising and the subject matter moving, I felt that the style of writing, where the reader discovers everything that has happened after the event itself, and often from a different viewpoint, somehow disconnected me from the emotion in the story. I would have preferred to find out more about the characters up-front, rather than discovering about them as the book progressed, so I could build a picture of them early on and then would have more of an emotional response when events unfolded. This doesn’t detract from the important issues this book raises and the keenness with which I pressed on to the end. But for such a powerful storyline, and the three first-person narratives giving us access to their innermost thoughts, I expected to feel more invested in their outcomes.

I think Celestial, at one point, even refers to the relationship as being like a sapling that didn’t have chance to root (or something along those lines!) and this is repeatedly juxtaposed by the rock solid, rooted connectedness shown by Big Roy and Olive’s relationship. Our feeling of not knowing them well enough is arguably, to an extent, the same as what Celestial feels towards Roy. Even right at the beginning their argument stems from Roy not having told her about his real father. Therefore, although I initially felt puzzled as to why I wasn’t overly moved by such a moving set of events, I now feel like my sense of emotional disconnect is not just that, instead it serves to illuminate further why their relationship could not be.

Circe by Madeline Miller

"In the house of Helios, god of the sun and mightiest of the Titans, a daughter is born. But Circe is a strange child--not powerful, like her father, nor viciously alluring like her mother. Turning to the world of mortals for companionship, she discovers that she does possess power--the power of witchcraft, which can transform rivals into monsters and menace the gods themselves.

Threatened, Zeus banishes her to a deserted island, where she hones her occult craft, tames wild beasts and crosses paths with many of the most famous figures in all of mythology, including the Minotaur, Daedalus and his doomed son Icarus, the murderous Medea, and, of course, wily Odysseus.

But there is danger, too, for a woman who stands alone, and Circe unwittingly draws the wrath of both men and gods, ultimately finding herself pitted against one of the most terrifying and vengeful of the Olympians. To protect what she loves most, Circe must summon all her strength and choose, once and for all, whether she belongs with the gods she is born from, or the mortals she has come to love.

With unforgettably vivid characters, mesmerizing language and page-turning suspense, Circe is a triumph of storytelling, an intoxicating epic of family rivalry, palace intrigue, love and loss, as well as a celebration of indomitable female strength in a man's world."


WHY PICK THIS BOOK?

January's book was a challenging real-life story so I have tried to find something a little different for February. Circe takes us back thousands of years into the world of myth, following Odysseus' adventures home after the Trojan War, but from the perspective of Circe, a sorceress. The timeless themes of love, family, healing, choice and fate will be examined in this award-winning read.


LUCY'S REVIEW

I haven’t read a “fantasy” style book in ages, and it took me longer than it should have to get into Circe, but it was definitely worth persisting. Circe is set in Ancient Greece and tells the story of an unusual goddess who doesn’t fit in among her own kind and instead is drawn towards mortals, seeking love and approval from them since she has not received it from the gods. The idea of a young person being born into a family they don’t fit with reminded me of Educated; both Tara and Circe struggle with their own kind and feel there is something else out there for them in an unfamiliar place; both find it hard to overcome the challenges the new place holds; both have to reconcile difficulties with their own family; and both eventually find a kind of harmony in their new life, having gained the strength to take it for themselves. Despite the mythological wrapper, Circe deals with contemporary themes and, at its core, is about a woman trying to make sense of the world and her place in it. It is a powerful read.

Circe’s status as a goddess adds emphasis to ordinary events in human life. The wrench she feels at her son growing up and entering the world is something felt by many parents, and I really felt her sadness as I read of Telegonus’ sailing off to find his father aged only 16. The fear Circe has of her son dying before her is one of the greatest fears of a parent’s life and the seeming inevitability of this, since her son is a demi-god and destined to die while she is immortal, heightens the emotion of this part of her story. The joy of having a son, someone to love her unconditionally in an existence where love has been lacking, turns into the greatest pain, that of losing a child. The fact that the greatest thing in her life can serve to increase the sadness she has experienced is heart-breaking and I felt that Miller highlighted this issue beautifully in the book.

The conclusion of the book again reminded me of contemporary issues and used the mythological world to make sense of them. In a family where she has always been mocked and used as a scapegoat, by the end of the book, Circe finally gathers the strength to stand up to her father and demand the end of her exile, showing great self-awareness and new-found confidence. In the final chapter, Miller doesn’t tell us whether Circe actually becomes mortal, but this doesn’t matter; the fact that she has come to terms with and is allowing herself to be true to her real self is empowering. Her characterisation throughout the book means that the reader goes through every trial with her and is glad that she has found her place at the end. If all readers come away questioning who they want to be in this world and having a keener awareness of their self-identity, as I certainly have, Miller will have triumphed.

JANUARY 2020

Educated by Tara Westover

“Tara Westover and her family grew up preparing for the End of Days but, according to the government, she didn’t exist. She hadn’t been registered for a birth certificate. She had no school records because she’d never set foot in a classroom, and no medical records because her father didn’t believe in hospitals.

As she grew older, her father became more radical and her brother more violent. At sixteen, Tara knew she had to leave home. In doing so she discovered both the transformative power of education, and the price she had to pay for it.”


WHY PICK THIS BOOK?

This was on one of Obama's book lists in 2019, and was recommended to me by a friend. It is a true story, written by Tara Westover about her own experiences. It is incredible to think of the changes she made in her life, from never attending school to being awarded a PhD from Cambridge. I'm expecting to be shocked and inspired by her story.


LUCY'S REVIEW

Education both makes Tara who she is and alienates her from everything she has ever known. It is easy to look at the title of the book, hear how she escaped from a controlling Mormon family and ended up gaining a PhD from Cambridge and imagine some happy ending where getting a good education is the thing that saves her. However, as the book unfolds, it is clear that things aren’t that simple. Her family cannot accept the new Tara nor do they acknowledge her views, eventually leading to estrangement. Regardless of how extreme their views or abusive their actions, Tara finds it hard to let go of her family and spends a large portion of her adult life seeking their approval, gripped by an irrational yet completely understandable desire to retain her relationship with them. For me, the gradual alienation of her family as she becomes more educated is one of the most poignant aspects of the book - she may have gained the freedom to form her own views but she lost important figures in the process so any sense of achievement at the end of the book is bittersweet.

For a long time, Tara made excuses for her family, justifying their actions and sometimes even misremembering events so as not to face the truth of what they were doing. To be critical of one’s family is perhaps the hardest thing, and the danger of accepting her family’s ways unquestioningly was brought out in the book. The damage that the family did to Tara and the impact the events of her childhood had on her mental health are evident throughout. The more times she is told that she is wrong, the more she believes it, and I was particularly saddened in the part where Tara constantly tries to convince herself that she had remembered events wrongly, putting her intellect and its reliability into question. I believe that education is an important factor in social change, and it is clear that it is her intellect that enables Tara to escape from Buck’s Peak and take control of her life. When this is called into question and suggested to be unreliable, then it seemed that the family’s hold on Tara would continue. By this point, the “character” of Tara was such that I felt that I understood who she was and how she might think, and it is a triumph on her part that I genuinely felt concern for her at this point.

I found this book to be simultaneously gripping and troubling. Tara’s reflective tone is not nostalgic and the text isn’t full of explicit life lessons or expectations of pity. She doesn’t even present the actions of her father and brother, which already elicit a strong response from the reader, as shockingly as she could. Her aim is not to expose her family and their ways and have the reader admire her for her courage and her journey. She writes with honesty and gives the reader a clear vision of her life on the mountain that somehow makes sense of her actions throughout the book. To say I enjoyed the book seems to disrespect its contents, but I certainly felt engaged and challenged by it. I have thought about it a lot over the past month and am glad to have chosen it as the first read of the year.
